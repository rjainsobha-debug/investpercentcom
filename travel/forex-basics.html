Forex Basics — How Currency Exchange Really Works

Every time you exchange money abroad, someone profits from the spread. Understand how forex rates work and you'll save thousands on your next international trip.

What is a Forex Rate?

A foreign exchange (forex) rate is the price at which one currency exchanges for another. When you see USD/INR = 83.5, it means 1 US Dollar buys 83.5 Indian Rupees.

Rates fluctuate second-by-second based on supply/demand, central bank policy, inflation differences, and geopolitical events.

Understanding the Interbank Rate vs Retail Rate

Interbank rate (Google, XE): USD/INR = 83.50. This is the "true" rate between banks.
What you get at a forex kiosk: ~81.50–82.00 (2–3% worse)
What you get at airport: ~79.00–80.00 (4–5% worse)
On ₹1 lakh exchange, that's ₹3,000–5,000 lost at airports vs best rate.

Bid, Ask, and Spread — The Dealer's Profit

  • Bid price: Price the dealer will BUY your foreign currency (you sell at this rate)
  • Ask price: Price the dealer will SELL foreign currency to you (you buy at this rate)
  • Spread: The difference between bid and ask — this is the dealer's profit
Spread Example

Dealer quotes: USD/INR Bid = 82.00 | Ask = 84.00. Spread = ₹2 per dollar.
If you exchange $1,000: You give ₹84,000 but the dealer could sell those dollars for ₹84,000. Their cost: ₹82,000. Dealer profit: ₹2,000 on your single exchange.

Tips for Smart Currency Exchange

  • Never exchange at the airport: Worst rates guaranteed — done by design as a captive audience
  • Book online 2–3 days early: Thomas Cook, BookMyForex — lock in rate + home delivery
  • Carry multiple options: Forex card (primary) + small cash + backup international credit card
  • Avoid dynamic currency conversion (DCC): When a foreign POS asks "Pay in INR?" — always say NO. Always pay in local currency.
  • Watch for hidden fees: Some forex cards charge monthly fee, ATM fee, or inactivity fee
DCC Warning: Dynamic Currency Conversion can add 5–7% markup on every card transaction abroad. Always choose to pay in the local currency, not INR.
