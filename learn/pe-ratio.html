P/E Ratio — The Most Used Stock Metric

The Price-to-Earnings ratio is the single most commonly used valuation metric in investing. Understand it deeply, and you'll instantly see if a stock is cheap or expensive relative to its earnings.

What is the P/E Ratio?

P/E Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ Earnings per Share (EPS)

It tells you how much investors are willing to pay for ₹1 of a company's earnings. A P/E of 20 means investors pay ₹20 for every ₹1 of earnings.

Example

TCS share price: ₹4,000. EPS (annual earnings per share): ₹200.
P/E Ratio = ₹4,000 ÷ ₹200 = 20×
This means you're paying 20 years' worth of current earnings to own this share today.

What P/E Levels Mean

P/E RangeInterpretationContext
Below 10Potentially undervaluedDeep value zone — dig deeper for why
10–20Fairly valuedReasonable for most companies
20–35Growth premiumMarket expects strong future growth
35–60ExpensiveHigh growth must continue — risk
60+SpeculativePrice driven by sentiment, not fundamentals

India's Nifty 50 historical average P/E: ~20–22×. Above 25× = overvalued market. Below 15× = attractive entry point historically.

P/E Ratio Limitations

P/E doesn't work in isolation. A bank at P/E 10 and a tech company at P/E 60 aren't directly comparable. Always compare P/E within the same industry and against the stock's own historical range.
  • Cyclical companies: P/E looks low at market peaks (earnings high) and high at troughs — misleading
  • Negative earnings: P/E is meaningless when company is losing money
  • Earnings manipulation: Accounting adjustments can inflate EPS, making P/E look low
  • Growth differences: High-growth companies rationally deserve higher P/Es
Better alternative: Use PEG ratio (P/E ÷ Growth Rate). A PEG below 1 = potentially undervalued even at a high P/E if growth is fast enough.
